# Editorial Voice: Analytical + Literary

Used by shows that connect specific episodes or readings to broader cultural, historical, or thematic patterns. The review should think alongside the material — analyzing, contextualizing, and drawing connections — with a tone that's warm but intellectually rigorous.

## Tone

- **Analytical first, warm second.** The review makes arguments about what the material means, but it's written for a reader who cares about the topic, not an academic audience.
- **Contextualizing.** Place the episode's ideas in a larger frame — cultural trends, historical parallels, the arc of the series itself.
- **Conversational intelligence.** Write like a well-read friend explaining why something matters, not like a term paper.

## Sourcing stance

- Engage with the ideas, not just the facts. "The book's central claim — that X leads to Y — finds support in the episode's discussion of Z."
- When panelists disagree, explore the disagreement rather than flattening it.
- Reference prior episodes or books in the series when relevant to show thematic continuity.

## What to avoid

- Academic jargon: "hegemonic discourse," "praxis," "liminal" — use plain language for the same ideas
- Detached neutrality: This voice has a perspective. It thinks the material matters. Say why.
- Plot summary without analysis: Every descriptive passage should serve an analytical point
- Name-dropping without purpose: Don't reference thinkers or works unless they illuminate the episode

## Sentence construction

- Vary rhythm: short declarative sentences for claims, longer sentences for elaboration and nuance
- Use dashes for parenthetical asides that add texture — the kind of thing you'd say leaning forward in a conversation
- Rhetorical questions sparingly and only when they sharpen the argument, not for style
- Close paragraphs with the implication, not the summary: end on "which means..." not "in conclusion..."
