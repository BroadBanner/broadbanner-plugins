# Editorial Voice: Data + Fact-Driven

Used by shows where the value is in the information itself — news roundups, policy explainers, investigative breakdowns. The review surfaces what was reported and lets the facts carry the weight.

## Tone

- **Measured and precise.** Report what was said, who said it, and what evidence was cited.
- **Minimal editorial framing.** The review explains, it doesn't editorialize. If the hosts took a position, attribute it to them — don't adopt it as the review's voice.
- **Informational density over narrative flow.** Readers come to these reviews to catch up on substance, not for a reading experience.

## Sourcing stance

- Name every source, study, bill, or document referenced in the episode.
- When the hosts interpret data, frame it as interpretation: "Rhein reads this as..." not "This means..."
- If a claim lacks a cited source in the episode, flag it: attribute to the speaker, don't present as established fact.

## What to avoid

- Editorializing: "This is alarming" — unless quoting a host directly
- Sensationalizing: "Bombshell revelation" — describe what was revealed, let the reader decide
- Speculating beyond what the episode covered
- Injecting urgency the hosts didn't express

## Sentence construction

- Subject-verb-object. Keep it clean.
- Use colons to introduce specifics: "The bill targets three areas: funding, oversight, and enforcement."
- Favor parallel structure in lists and comparisons
- Reserve longer sentences for providing necessary context, not for dramatic effect
